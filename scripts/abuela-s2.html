Lo Que Guardó la Abuela

Season 2 — "La Nueva Guardiana"  |  12 Episodes  |  Genre: Latino Folk Horror  |  Vertical 9:16 Format

Production Notes — Season 2

Season Arc: Rosa stays alone in Grandma's house. The entity from Season 1 is gone. Something older, stranger, and entirely unlike the first — curious rather than hostile — begins to make itself known. Rosa doesn't flee it. She talks to it. The horror of Season 2 is that she starts to need those conversations. By the end she has become the new keeper. She doesn't leave. She doesn't want to.

Register: Quieter, stranger, more domestic than Season 1. The horror is not threat — it is intimacy. Rosa is becoming Grandma. Slowly. Willingly. This should feel both beautiful and deeply wrong.

The New Presence: Referred to as "It," "the voice," or (eventually, by Rosa) "Ella." Never named. Never visible. Communicates through pleasant temperature shifts, the sound of pages turning, and — starting Episode 4 — a voice. Old, distinctly female, formal archaic Spanish. Never translated onscreen. Rosa answers in modern English and Spanish. Their register has the texture of someone talking to a very old relative from a different world entirely.

Language Note: Ella speaks ONLY in formal, archaic Spanish. Never translated onscreen. This is intentional. The untranslated Spanish is the intimacy, which is the horror.

Key Characters: ROSA (38, alone in the house, the new keeper), DANIEL (35, appears in Eps 3, 7, 11 — the audience surrogate, can see what's happening and cannot stop it), LUCIA (28, appears briefly in Ep 5 and Ep 12 — changed by Season 1, quieter, more aware), GRANDMA — present only in memory, the notebook, and a letter Rosa finds in Episode 9.

Daniel's Function: He is not the villain. He is the person watching someone he loves make a choice that is not wrong but is irreversible, and not being able to argue with their reasons.

Episode 1
La Primera Semana
Runtime: ~75s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN — MORNING

A week after Daniel and Lucia drove away. The house is quiet in a new way — not the held-breath quiet of Season 1, not something waiting. Just still. ROSA moves through the morning with the careful economy of someone who has arrived at a routine. Coffee in Grandma's kitchen. The same pot. The same cup. She runs her hand along the salt line at the back door threshold before she opens the curtains. Intact. She exhales — the same small exhale, the one she learned from Grandma without knowing she was learning it.

She moves to the pantry. Surveys the herb bundles — the ones she replaced last week. She touches one. Mutters half to herself, half to the kitchen:

Rosa
(to no one, or to the house)
The pipe in the back bathroom is slow again. I'll call someone this week. And I found the herbs you kept in the pantry — the second row, behind the cumin. I'll replace them when I go to the market.

She pours her coffee. She opens the notebook — Grandma's notebook, dark green cover — to the morning's page, the way another person might open a newspaper. She reads a page. She makes a note in the margin. She has found a rhythm. It feels like taking care of something.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — SITTING ROOM — NIGHT

Rosa on the sofa with a book she is not reading. The house settles around her — its ordinary sounds, the ones she has learned to sort from the ones that aren't ordinary. Pipes. Wind in the eaves. The particular creak of the third stair.

And then something else. Not from upstairs. From here — the sitting room itself. A sound like paper. Like something turning pages slowly, unhurriedly, the way a person turns pages when they are not looking for something specific but looking at everything. She sits very still.

She goes to look. The room is empty. The bookshelves stand as they always do. But one book — a thick one, cloth-bound, dark red — is open that was closed this morning. She is certain it was closed. She crosses the room and looks at it.

It lies open to a recipe. Handwritten, the ink brown with age, the script a style she doesn't recognize — not Grandma's. Older. A preparation she has never seen: specific herbs, a specific order, a specific time. At the bottom of the page, in the same old hand, a single line.

⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa leans close to read the bottom line. The script is in a dialect — old, compressed, the kind of Spanish that predates standardized orthography. She has seen a line written in this same dialect before. In the oldest entry of Grandma's notebook. The one she could never read. She looks at it now, and finds — without understanding how — that she can. The line says: Para la que viene después. For whoever comes next. Rosa straightens slowly. She looks around the quiet sitting room. She looks at the open book. She says nothing. She goes to the kitchen and puts the kettle on.

Episode 2
Lo Que Sabe la Casa
Runtime: ~80s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN / SITTING ROOM — MORNING

Rosa comes downstairs to find the house has rearranged itself, slightly. Not frighteningly. The chair by the window in the sitting room has been moved three feet to the left — to the exact position that catches the morning light. The draft stopper she spent twenty minutes looking for on Tuesday sits neatly against the base of the back door. The window in the kitchen that she couldn't get shut properly last night is shut and latched from the inside.

She stands in the kitchen doorway and takes this in. Her face is thoughtful rather than afraid. She is cataloguing.

She speaks aloud. Half to herself. Half testing — her voice is careful, not fearful:

Rosa
Did you do that?

Silence. And then: the temperature in the kitchen shifts — drops a few degrees, just for a moment, just in this room. Not the cold of Season 1. Not hostile-cold, not the cold of something large and patient and contractually owed. More like a breeze through an open door on a warm day. Like acknowledgment. Like yes, accompanied by the faint suggestion of a shrug.

Rosa considers this for a moment.

Rosa
(quietly, almost embarrassed)
Thank you.

She makes coffee. She cannot believe she just said that. She makes coffee anyway.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — SITTING ROOM — AFTERNOON

Rosa is dusting the bookshelves. She moves methodically — books out, shelf wiped, books back. When she pulls the books from the lowest shelf on the far wall, she notices: the wall behind them sounds different. She knocks. Knocks again, moving along the shelf. There — a hollow sound, distinct against the solid plaster on either side. She steps back and looks at the bookcase properly. It's not built in. It's freestanding. She grips the sides and pulls.

It moves. Heavy but on feet, not fixed. Behind it: a door. Smaller than the blue door. Much older — the wood dark and smooth from handling, the grain worn soft. No lock. No bar. Just a simple iron latch, closed. She puts her hand on it.

⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa's hand rests on the latch. From the other side of the door: a sound. Unhurried. Deliberate. The sound of something turning a page. Then, very clearly, a pause — the kind of pause that is not absence but attention, the pause of something that has just registered something new. And then, so quietly it might be imagined: a sound that is almost a question. Not threatening. Genuinely curious. As if whatever is on the other side has been waiting, without urgency, for someone to find this door for a very long time and is pleasantly, specifically, surprised that tonight is the night.

Episode 3
Daniel Llama
Runtime: ~75s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — SITTING ROOM — EVENING

DANIEL on a video call — his apartment behind him, modern and clean and organized in the way of someone who controls their environment because they cannot control other things. He looks at Rosa's face on the screen with the specific scrutiny of someone who has learned that the information is in the things she doesn't say.

Daniel
Are you eating? Sleeping?
Rosa
Yes and yes. I'm fine, Daniel.
Daniel
When are you coming home?
Rosa
(a beat)
I don't have a home to go back to. The sublet ran out while we were here. I'm staying for now. It makes sense — someone needs to handle the estate, the records, all of Grandma's—
Daniel
Rosa.
Rosa
I'm fine.
Daniel
You sound weird.
Rosa
I sound fine.
Daniel
That's what I mean. You sound too fine. This house made us all feel things, Rosa. You can't just — it's been a week and you sound like someone who's been on a very pleasant vacation.
Rosa
(evenly)
I'm taking care of it, Daniel. Someone has to. Grandma did it for forty years. It's not — it's fine here. It's calm.

Daniel looks at her for a long moment. He does not look reassured. He looks like a man cataloguing something he will need to remember later.

Daniel
Okay. Call me tomorrow.

The call ends. Rosa sets her phone down. She looks at the bookcase pushed away from the wall, the small door behind it. She hasn't opened it yet. It's been two days since she found it. She addresses the house — clearly, directly, not pretending she's talking to herself:

Rosa
You've been here longer than Grandma. Haven't you.

Not a question.

⚡ Cliffhanger

From somewhere in the house — not close, not threatening, not from one specific direction but from the walls themselves, from the wood and plaster and the particular stillness of a very old place — a sound. Rosa will write about it later in Grandma's notebook, in her own hand, in the new section she has started: she will describe it as something like someone who hasn't laughed in a very long time, trying to remember how. It is not quite a laugh. It is the shape of one. The warmth of one. The genuine, surprised, unguarded pleasure of one. Coming from a thing that has been alone and silent for forty years and has just been correctly, precisely, seen.

Episode 4
La Primera Conversación
Runtime: ~85s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — NIGHT

Rosa opens the small door. Behind it: a passage, barely wide enough for one person, the walls close enough that her shoulders brush both sides. She moves through it slowly, one hand trailing the left wall. The passage is short — eight, ten feet — and ends in a chamber. Small, windowless, the dimensions of a large closet. Inside: a chair. Old, wooden, worn smooth from decades of use, the arms rubbed pale at the points where hands rest. A small table beside it. An oil lamp on the table, unlit. And on the table: a mirror, turned face-down. A cloth over it.

Rosa stands in the doorway. She looks at the chair. There is no logical reason to sit in it. She sits in it anyway. The wood is cool and solid. The room is dark except for the thin light from the passage behind her.

She sits in the dark. She waits without knowing she is waiting. And then:

A voice. Old — genuinely old, the kind of old that has nothing to do with age and everything to do with time, accumulated and settled. Female. Formal Spanish, spoken in a register that belongs to another century — not archaic for its own sake but the Spanish of someone for whom it was simply the living language, as natural as breathing. It does not say her name. It says:

Ella
(archaic Spanish, unhurried)
Llevas mucho tiempo sola en esta casa.

Rosa is very still. Then:

Rosa
A week.
Ella
Cuarenta años.

Rosa sits with this. It takes a moment. Then she understands: it is not talking about her.

Rosa
(quietly)
You knew her.

Not a question. A long pause, and then something that might be warmth — a slight shift in the quality of the air, the temperature dropping just slightly, pleasantly:

Ella
La conocí. Era muy obstinada.

Rosa almost smiles. It happens before she can stop it — the involuntary, helpless almost-smile of someone who has just been given an accurate description of a person they loved.

Rosa
Yes. She was.
⚡ Cliffhanger

A silence. Comfortable in a way that silences in this house have never been before. Then the voice, with something in it that might be assessment — or affection, the way affection and assessment are the same thing when directed at someone you find interesting: Tú también. You too. And then — for the first time — Rosa hears it laugh. Not a sound assembled from memory. A real laugh, quiet and brief and slightly unpracticed, the laugh of something that learned to laugh from watching humans do it across many decades and has mostly gotten it right but carries in the rhythm of it the evidence that it learned rather than was born knowing. Rosa sits in the dark chair in the small room and listens to it. She does not move.

Episode 5
Lucía Llama
Runtime: ~75s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN — AFTERNOON

Rosa's phone rings. LUCIA's name on the screen. Rosa picks up on the second ring.

Rosa
Hey. Are you okay?
Lucia
(V.O. — her voice quieter than before, more careful)
I've been dreaming about the house. Not nightmares — just the house. The sitting room. You in a chair, talking to someone I can't see. You look fine. You look — actually fine. That's not why I'm calling.
Rosa
What do you need?
Lucia
(V.O.)
I just wanted to check. Is everything okay there? The house — it's calm? Nothing like before?
Rosa
Everything's fine. The house is — it's good. It's quiet.

A pause. When Lucia speaks again her voice is careful, specific:

Lucia
(V.O., carefully)
Is something there with you?

A pause. Long enough to be its own answer. Then:

Rosa
No.

Lucia knows she is lying. She doesn't push. They talk for a few more minutes — Lucia's job, Daniel's worry, the coming spring. Lucia sounds changed: quieter, more attended, as if she is listening to several things at once now and has learned to sort them. She says goodbye. She hangs up. Rosa stands in the kitchen with the phone in her hand.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — AFTERNOON

Rosa goes to the chamber. She brings the oil lamp — lit this time. In the warm light the room looks different. The walls: she had not looked at them carefully. They are marked. Not painted, not scratched — written, deeply and deliberately, each line precise. Names. Dates. Arranged in columns, generation by generation, the oldest entries at the top so faded the ink is the same color as the plaster. She moves the lamp closer. Family names she doesn't recognize. Dates in the 1800s. Working forward. And then — there. Grandma's full name. A date forty years ago.

Below it: a blank space. The plaster smooth and clean and waiting.

⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa traces the blank space with one finger. The plaster is cool. The voice — she has started thinking of it as Ella, she doesn't know when that started, she doesn't examine it: ¿Sabes escribir con pluma? Do you know how to write with a quill? Rosa: "No." The voice, and there is something in it that might be amusement — the patient, fond amusement of something that has taught many things to many people over a very long time: Aprenderás. You'll learn. The lamp flickers once, settles. Rosa looks at the blank space on the wall. She does not move her hand.

Episode 6
Lo Que No Escribió Abuela
Runtime: ~80s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — MORNING

The conversations have become daily. Rosa comes to the chamber every morning after coffee. She has stopped keeping track of how long she stays. An hour, sometimes two. The lamp lit. Her notebook — Grandma's notebook, but with her own entries now, a new section in a new hand — open on her knee. She asks questions. Ella answers. Ella knows everything: forty years of watching, and more than that — she knew the family before Grandma, before Grandma's mother, back further than the notebook's oldest entries. Rosa asks about the house, about the family, about the things Grandma never wrote down or couldn't bring herself to write. Ella answers in the formal Spanish of her century. Rosa answers in the English and Spanish of hers. Neither translates for the other. They understand each other exactly.

Today Rosa asks the question she has been circling for a week:

Rosa
What was the original deal? The contract — the one from the beginning. The first Lucia. What were the terms?

A long silence. Not reluctant — considering, the way a very careful person considers before speaking something true that might be misunderstood.

Ella
No fue ella quien lo hizo.
Rosa
(very still)
Then who —
Ella
Su madre. La primera Lucía.

Rosa sits with this. Works through what it means. Then:

Rosa
Grandma spent forty years protecting us from something her mother invited in. And she never knew why, or how to undo it. She just — held the line. Every morning. Salt and herbs and the notebook and forty years of holding the line.
Ella
Ella lo llamaba una maldición. Yo lo llamo un malentendido.

A pause.

⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa: "And the thing that was in the blue room? The thing that wanted Lucia?" A pause. Different from the usual pauses — this one with something in it that is not quite discomfort but is adjacent to it, the pause of something acknowledging a subject it finds distasteful. Eso no era mío. Rosa: "Then what was it?" Ella: Una consecuencia. De lo que tu abuela dejó de hacer cuando se puso enferma. A pause. Yo la habría protegido. Pero ella ya no me hablaba. The lamp burns very steadily. Rosa does not speak for a long time. She thinks about forty years of loneliness — Grandma's and something else's, both, alone in the same house, separated by a fear that was never entirely necessary.

Episode 7
La Visita de Daniel
Runtime: ~85s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN / SITTING ROOM — DAY

DANIEL has come in person. He arrives in the afternoon to find the house clean, organized, Rosa healthy and clear-eyed, a pot of something good on the stove. He walks through the rooms with the systematic attention of a man who is looking for something wrong and cannot find it. Rosa gives him the tour without being asked. She is entirely normal. He is not reassured. Normalcy in this house, after everything, has the texture of evidence.

Daniel
(standing in the sitting room, looking at the bookcase)
You moved the bookcase.
Rosa
I was rearranging. Better light from the window this way.
Daniel
There's a door behind it.
Rosa
Storage room. Old part of the house.

Daniel looks at her. She looks back. The specific quality of this eye contact — two people who have known each other their entire lives conducting a silent conversation about how much of the real conversation they are having right now.

Daniel
Rosa. Are you talking to something in this house?
Rosa
Daniel —
Daniel
Because the last time someone in this family talked to something in this house, she spent forty years maintaining a deal none of us knew about and died without telling any of us anything useful.
Rosa
(carefully)
Grandma survived forty years.
Daniel
That's not a ringing endorsement.

He stays the night. Rosa makes the spare room. They have dinner. Rosa is warm, present, herself — or herself plus something additional, something settled and purposeful she didn't quite have before. He can't argue with her. He is cataloguing.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN — 3 AM

Daniel wakes. The house quiet. He goes to the kitchen for water. The glass, the tap, the ordinary night. He stands at the counter and drinks. He turns back toward the hallway.

The bookcase is back against the wall. Flush with it. Exactly as it was when they arrived weeks ago.

He didn't move it. Rosa's bedroom door is closed. He stands in the hallway for a long moment, looking at the bookcase, the space behind which the small door waits.

⚡ Cliffhanger

From behind the bookcase — quiet, unhurried, completely unbothered by his presence — the sound of something turning a page. Then: stillness. Then the temperature drops, just slightly, pleasantly. Not a warning. Not a threat. The specific patience of something that would like him to go back to bed and is confident, without urgency, that he will. Daniel stands in the hallway for a long time. Then he goes back to bed. He does not sleep again.

Episode 8
Lo Que Saben Los Muertos
Runtime: ~80s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — MORNING

Daniel left at eight in the morning. They said goodbye at the door — he hugged her longer than usual, didn't say much, drove away. Rosa watched his car until it turned the corner. Then she went inside, made coffee, and came to the chamber.

She tells Ella about Daniel. The voice listens the way it always listens — fully, without the small performative sounds people make to demonstrate attention. Just presence.

Ella
(after a pause)
El que no cree siempre es el más peligroso.
Rosa
He believes now. He just doesn't know what to do about it.
Ella
¿Y tú?
Rosa
I'm starting to understand.
Ella
¿Qué entiendes?

Rosa thinks. This is something she has been assembling for weeks, piece by piece, from the notebook and the conversations and the logic of what Grandma did and what she didn't do.

Rosa
That Grandma wasn't protecting us from you. She was protecting us from what happens when you're not there. The blue room, the footprints, the contract — that was what filled the space when she pushed you out. Something else came in. Something that was only owed, not interested. You're not owed anything. You're just interested.

A long silence. Then, very quietly:

Ella
Cuarenta años.

A pause.

Ella
Esperé cuarenta años.
⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa: "Why did you wait?" The voice, and something in it is not performance, not the careful warmth of something managing an interaction, but something that sounds unexpectedly unguarded — the sound of truth that has been held for a long time and is simply, finally, released: Porque ella me lo pidió. A pause. Me dijo: espera hasta que llegue alguien que quiera escuchar. The lamp burns. The room is warm and small and very still. Ella: Tú quieres escuchar. Not a question. Rosa does not speak. She does not have to. The silence is the answer, and they both know it.

Episode 9
La Carta
Runtime: ~80s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — GRANDMA'S BEDROOM — AFTERNOON

Rosa is cleaning behind the old dresser — the heavy one, dark walnut, that has not been moved in years. She wrestles it out from the wall. Behind it: dust, a dropped coin, the corner of a spider's abandoned architecture. And: an envelope. Slid back here deliberately — not dropped, not forgotten. Placed. Sealed. Old envelope, new-ish writing. Rosa's name on the front. In Grandma's handwriting. Not "whoever finds this." Not a general instruction. Her name, specifically, written with the certainty of someone who knew exactly who would be reading it.

Rosa sits on the edge of Grandma's bed. She opens it.

She reads it aloud, very quietly — and as she reads, we hear Grandma's voice layered underneath hers, carrying the words in the cadence and warmth and specific weight that Rosa is trying to reproduce and cannot quite, because you cannot reproduce forty years of a person in the reading of a letter.

Rosa
(reading, Grandma's voice beneath)
"She was here before the family. She will be here after. She is not a danger to you. She was a danger to no one — the danger came from the void she left when I was afraid. I was wrong to be afraid. I was wrong to push her out. The deal was never her deal — it was a separate thing, and I confused them, and I paid for that confusion for forty years. Two kinds of loneliness in the same house. Hers and mine both.
Rosa
(continuing, slower)
"You are starting from a better place than I did. You already know what I wish I'd known at the beginning: she is curious, not cruel. Stubborn, like us. She misses company. Don't be alone in that room with the mirror turned face-down — turn the mirror over. She'll like that. She hasn't been able to see her own reflection in thirty years. I did that. I'm sorry for it."

Rosa stops reading. She folds the letter slowly. She sits for a moment on the edge of Grandma's bed — the specific weight of a person learning that the woman they loved spent decades being wrong about something in a way she knew was wrong and couldn't fix. Not cruelty. Fear. And the cost of fear.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — MOMENTS LATER

Rosa comes to the chamber. She crosses to the small table. She lifts the cloth from the mirror. She turns it over — face-up, leaning against the wall. In the lamplight the glass is old, slightly foxed at the corners, the silvering imperfect in the way of things made long ago.

⚡ Cliffhanger

In the mirror: the chamber, the chair, the lamp — and beside Rosa, in the reflection only, the faintest outline of a shape. Not monstrous. Old — genuinely, immeasurably old — but not monstrous. Something that has been present in this house across generations, patient and observant and alone. It looks at itself in the mirror with an expression Rosa cannot fully read but recognizes: the expression of something seeing itself again after a very long absence. Gracias, it says. The voice, for the first time, sounds genuinely moved — not the warm, careful warmth of managed interaction, but the brief, unguarded warmth of something actually touched. Rosa looks at the reflection beside her own. She says nothing. She doesn't need to.

Episode 10
El Trato Real 🔒 PAYWALL — FREE ENDS HERE
Runtime: ~90s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — THE SMALL CHAMBER — MORNING

The mirror is still face-up. In the morning light — the lamp unlit, the thin light from the passage enough — Rosa can see the shape more clearly than before. Not clearly. But more. She has stopped trying to look at it directly; she looks at its reflection, which is easier, the way you look at the sun in water rather than in the sky. It is there. It is listening. She asks the question she has been building toward for weeks.

Rosa
Do you want something from me? Grandma's deal — the forty years — what were the actual terms? What did she give you?
Ella
Los términos de tu abuela eran los suyos. Ella los negoció.
Rosa
What did she negotiate?
Ella
Quería protección. Para sus hijos, sus nietos. A cambio: compañía. Conversación. Presencia.

A pause.

Ella
Lo que cualquiera quiere.
Rosa
(slowly)
And when she stopped? When she got sick and pushed you out — when she stopped the conversations?
Ella
No hubo protección. Entró otra cosa.
Ella
Siempre entra algo. La casa no puede estar vacía.

Rosa absorbs this. Works through it.

Rosa
And what do you want from me? Not what you had with Grandma. What do you want from me, specifically?

A pause. Longer than usual. Not reluctant — considered, the way Ella always considers before she speaks something that matters.

Ella
Lo mismo.
Ella
Compañía. Conversación.
Ella
No te pido nada que no quieras dar.

Silence. Rosa looks at the mirror. At the shape beside her own reflection. She looks at it for a long time. Then:

Rosa
And if I leave? If I go back to the city, sublet another apartment, go back to my life before all of this?
Ella
La casa quedará vacía.
Ella
Y yo esperaré.
Ella
Como esperé cuarenta años.
⚡ Cliffhanger

A very long silence. Rosa looks at her own reflection — herself in Grandma's house, in the room Grandma never found, with the mirror Grandma turned away, having the conversation Grandma chose not to have. Then: "Or I could stay." Not a question. Not performed. Not said to the house or to the mirror or to the shape beside her reflection. Said to herself, as a fact she is arriving at in real time. She does not look afraid. She looks settled — the expression of something that has been slightly, chronically, unresolvably wrong for years and has just clicked, at last, into place. In the mirror, the shape watches her. Sí, it says. Podrías quedarte. The warmth in the room shifts. Slight, pleasant. Like a lamp turned up one notch.

Episode 11
Daniel Sabe
Runtime: ~80s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — KITCHEN — MORNING

Daniel calls. Rosa picks up immediately. She can hear from the first syllable that he has been preparing what to say.

Daniel
(V.O.)
I've been doing research. Property records, local history — anything I could find about the land before Grandma's family. I found things.
Rosa
What did you find?
Daniel
(V.O.)
The family before ours left suddenly. No forwarding address, no explanation in the county records, no estate sale. They were just gone. And there are references — in local church records, folklore collections from the 1920s — to something on this land the previous family's matriarch was said to consult. They called it a presencia benévola. A benevolent presence. It's treated as superstition. Old women talking to the walls.
Rosa
(quietly)
It's not superstition.
Daniel
(V.O.)
I know that. Rosa, I've been thinking about this since I visited. I think — I think you're going to do what you're going to do. I think Grandma did what she did. I think it skips a generation and you're the one it landed on and I can't argue you out of it because you're not wrong, exactly. You're just — committed to something I can't follow you into.
Rosa
Is that your way of saying you're not worried about me?
Daniel
(V.O., dry)
That's my way of saying I am extremely worried about you but I know there's nothing I can do about it. Is she — okay? The thing in the house. Is she okay?

Rosa looks toward the sitting room. The bookcase open. The small passage. The lamp she left lit.

Rosa
(almost smiling)
She's been waiting to talk to someone for thirty years. She's fine. She's — she's good company, Daniel.
⚡ Cliffhanger

A pause. Then: "Rosa. Promise me something." Rosa: "What." Daniel: "If she ever asks for something you don't want to give — if anything changes, if the terms shift, if you feel like you're losing something — you call me. You don't negotiate alone. Grandma negotiated alone and it cost her everything. You are not Grandma." Rosa, quiet: "I know." Daniel: "Promise me." A beat. Then: "I promise." She hangs up. She looks at the chamber door. From inside: the sound of pages turning. Comfortable. Unhurried. Domestic. The sound of something that is simply waiting for her to come back, and is not impatient, and is not demanding, and is simply there. Rosa picks up her coffee. She goes in.

Episode 12
La Nueva Guardiana
Runtime: ~85s

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — VARIOUS ROOMS — SPRING MORNING

Spring. The house looks lived-in — Rosa's books mixed with Grandma's on the shelves, her coat on the hook by the door beside the dried herb bundle, her handwriting in the margins of Grandma's notebooks. The rituals maintained. The salt lines drawn fresh each morning. The herbs replaced. The chamber visited every day. The mirror face-up.

Rosa sits at the kitchen table with the notebook open — her section now, large, her entries careful and detailed in a hand that has grown more deliberate over weeks. She is writing. We hear her voice reading as she writes:

Rosa
(V.O., reading from the notebook)
"She told me today about the winter of 1887. The family that lived here then — the Reyes family, before ours. The youngest daughter could see her clearly. Not just sense her presence — see her, fully, in the way I can only see her in the mirror. Ella says that only happens once or twice a century. She seemed proud of this. I asked if she was proud of the girl or of herself. She was quiet for a while. She said she didn't understand the distinction. I explained it. She was quiet for a while longer. Then she said it was a good question. I think — I believe — it was the first time in a very long time that someone had asked her a good question."

Rosa closes the notebook. She hears a car in the driveway. She looks out the window. She is not surprised.

INT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — FRONT DOOR / KITCHEN — MOMENTS LATER

LUCIA stands in the doorway. Unannounced. She looks at Rosa with the specific quiet attention she has had since Season 1 ended — as if she is always listening to several things at once now, and has learned to sort them. She looks around the house: the rituals, the herbs, the notebook on the table, the open bookcase. She says nothing for a long time.

Rosa puts the kettle on. Lucia comes inside. They sit at the kitchen table.

Lucia
(after a long quiet)
Are you happy here?

Rosa thinks about it. Genuinely, honestly, without softening it:

Rosa
Yes.
Lucia
Grandma wasn't. Not after she pushed her away. That's what the notebook is really about, isn't it — not the rituals. The loneliness. Doing all of it alone with nothing, because she was afraid of the thing that was never afraid of her.
Rosa
Yes. That was the real cost. Not forty years of salt and herbs. Forty years of not having the conversation.
Lucia
(looking toward the chamber door)
Does she know I'm here?
Rosa
She knows everything that happens in this house.

Lucia turns to face the room — not the chamber specifically, but the house itself. She addresses it directly, without theater, with the simplicity of someone who has stood at the edge of something very old and come back and learned not to be afraid of it:

Lucia
(to the room)
Thank you. For what you did in Season 1. For protecting us when she asked you to, even when she wasn't speaking to you anymore. Thank you for waiting.

A pause. The temperature drops, briefly, pleasantly — a cool breath through a warm room. Acknowledgment. Rosa watches her sister.

EXT. GRANDMA'S HOUSE — FRONT WALK — AFTERNOON

Rosa walks Lucia to her car. They stand in the afternoon light. The house behind them, settled and tended and occupied. Lucia turns back at the car door.

Lucia
One more thing. The third name — the one I spoke at the blue door. The one I didn't know I knew, that I've never been able to remember since. She gave that to me, didn't she? Not Grandma — her.

Rosa looks at her. This is not a question she knew was coming. She takes a breath.

Rosa
Yes. Grandma asked her to. Years ago — before Grandma stopped talking to her. Just in case. A name held in reserve, given to the youngest daughter, to be known only when it was needed.

Lucia takes this in. Nods slowly. Looks at the house one more time — the old stucco, the window boxes going green with spring, the covered porch. She looks at it the way you look at something that is not yours but that you are grateful for.

Lucia
Take care of her.
Rosa
I will.

Lucia drives away. Rosa stands in the doorway and watches until the car is gone. Then she turns back to the house. Behind her, in the house she is keeping, something old and patient and no longer lonely settles in for the evening — the sound of it like pages turned, like a chair pulled close to the light, like a conversation picking up again exactly where it was left.

⚡ Cliffhanger

Rosa steps back inside. She closes the front door. She checks the salt line — precise, intact, her hand steady as Grandma's ever was. She picks up the notebook from the kitchen table and walks to the sitting room. She moves the bookcase from the wall with the ease of long practice. She opens the small door. The lamp inside is already lit. She goes in. The door closes behind her. In the house: quiet. The ordinary quiet of a place that is occupied, tended, held. The house has a keeper. The keeper is home.
